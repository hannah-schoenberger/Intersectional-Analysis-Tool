    Positional Complexity Which/how many social positions e.g. gender identity beyond the binary are included in each category?

    Methodological Challenges

    • Limitations of the scale level in regression approaches prioritize binary operationalization or one-dimensional polarization.1,2,3,4
    • Predetermined operationalization in secondary analysis.5

    Potential Approaches

    • Multidimensional operationalization.6,7,12
    • Applying best practices from unitary approaches.6,8,9,10
    • Fostering intersectionality-sensitive innovation of operationalisation in large databases and official statistics.11,12
    Further readings
    1. Boyd, Alicia E. (2021): Quantitative Intersectional Data (QUINTA): A #metoo Case Study. https://via.library.depaul.edu/cdm_etd/36 (30.09.25) discusses challenges related to non-binary gender variables.
    2. Harari, Lexi/Lee, Chioun (2021): Intersectionality in quantitative health disparities research: A systematic review of challenges and limitations in empirical studies. In: Social Science & Medicine 277 (May), 113876. doi: 10.1016/j.socscimed.2021.113876 review showing the lack of non-binary operationalization.
    3. Bauer, Greta R. (2014): Incorporating Intersectionality Theory into Population Health Research Methodology: Challenges and the Potential to Advance Health Equity. In: Social Science & Medicine 110 (June), 10–17. doi: 10.1016/j.socscimed.2014.03.022 identifying challenges in interpretation - linear vs. logistic regression.
    4. Bauer, Greta R./Churchill, Siobhan M./Mahendran, Mayuri/Walwyn, Chantel/Lizotte, Daniel/Villa-Rueda, Alma Angelica (2021): Intersectionality in Quantitative Research: A Systematic Review of Its Emergence and Applications of Theory and Methods. review showing frequent use of big datasets ( mostly secondary analysis) + lack of complex operationalization
    5. Rouhani, Setareh (2014): Intersectionality-informed quantitative research: A primer. Burnaby: Institute for Intersectionality Research & Policy (Simon Fraser University). discusses challenges of secondary data analysis.

    11. Baumann, Anne-Luise/Egenberger, Vera/Supik, Linda (2018): Erhebung von Antidiskriminierungsdaten in repräsentativen Wiederholungsbefragungen. Bestandsaufnahme und Entwicklungsmöglichkeiten.     Inter-categorical Complexity Which/how many categories e.g. gender, race, class, disability... are included in the analysis?

    Methodological Challenges

    • Overfitting
    • Exponential increase of predictors in regression models with interaction terms1,2,3

    Potential Approaches

    • Rejecting the principle of parsimony in intersectional analysis4,5,6
    • Theory-driven or semi-theoretical variable selection7
    • MAIHDA1

    Intra-categorical Complexity Which constellations of social positions e.g. Black lesbian women, white non-binary academics, disabled Romnja... become visible at the intersections?

    Methodological Challenges

    • Small sample size in subgroup analysis.1,2,3,4
    • Asymmetric sample-size distribution.1,2,3,4

    Potential Approaches

    • Weighting and shrinking methods.2,6
    • Oversampling.2,7,8,3,9
    • Data pooling.2
    • Reporting non-significant results.5/higher p-value cut-off2,6

    Anti-/Meta-categorical Complexity What similarities and continuities exist between differentiated elements? How are boundaries drawn and differences reproduced?

    Methodological Challenges

    • Strong embedding of categorical thinking.1,2
    • Naturalization of sociodemographic variables (see also core concept ‘power’)

    Potential Approaches

    • Discriminatory accuracy.1,3,4
    • Reporting contradictions.5,6
    • Reflection and transparent reporting of classification processes and data used.7,8,6

    Multilevel Complexity Which levels of analysis are addressed? At which societal level are the analysed elements situated?

    Methodological Challenges

    • Limited experience and best practices in empirical research with multilevel approaches; current research often focuses primarily on the individual or identity level.1,2,3,4,5

    Potential Approaches

    • Including units of analysis located on different levels.1
    • Multilevel models.2,6,7,8
    • Hierarchical/nested data structures.9
    Further readings
    1. Bowleg, Lisa/Bauer, Greta (2016): Invited Reflection: Quantifying Intersectionality. In: Psychology of Women Quarterly 40 (3), 337–341. doi: 10.1177/0361684316654282 discusses multilevel modelling e.g. aggregated individual data
    2. Bauer, Greta R. (2014): Incorporating Intersectionality Theory into Population Health Research Methodology: Challenges and the Potential to Advance Health Equity. In: Social Science & Medicine 110 (June), 10–17. doi: 10.1016/j.socscimed.2014.03.022 discussing intersectional analysis on different levels: e.g. identities, positions, processes, policies
    3. Hancock, Ange-Marie (2013): Empirical Intersectionality: A Tale of Two Approaches. In: UC Irvine Law Review 3, 259–296. doi: 10.2139/ssrn.2290294 discussing challenges to measure different domains/levels of intersectionality empirically– referring to crenshaws typology: structural, political, representational

    Notes
      The tool provides selected examples of methodological challenges identified in the literature on quantitative intersectional analysis, together with approaches proposed to address them. These examples are derived from the literature analysis underlying the development of the tool. While not exhaustive, they illustrate how the framework can be applied to systematically reflect on methodological concerns. Readers are encouraged to use them as a starting point for further methodological discussion and for expanding the analytical toolkit of (quantitative) intersectional research. Although the examples presented here primarily focus on quantitative approaches, an analogous application of the framework to qualitative research may also prove fruitful.
