# Power

<div class="hero-quote">
“[Intersectional] frameworks that take power relations into account <span class="quote-ellipsis">[…]</span> raise new questions about global social inequality.”
<span class="quote-author">Collins &amp; Bilge (2020, 36)</span>
</div>

<p>
Intersectional research examines interlocking systems of power and their co-production of social inequalities (Collins 2019, 46; Collins/Bilge 2020, 35, 191). These interwoven structures operate across multiple levels of society, forming a multidimensional matrix of domination. Within this matrix, power is continually negotiated and reconfigured through processes of oppression and resistance (Collins/Bilge 2020, 36, 192).
</p>

<p>
However, translating this understanding into quantitative methodologies poses significant challenges. Socio-demographic and administrative variables are frequently naturalized and treated as self-explanatory (Supik 2017, 203), while complex, power-sensitive operationalizations are rarely available.
</p>

<p>
The <a href="guiding/">guiding</a> and <a href="methodological/">methodological</a>  questions on power support a stronger theoretical grounding of quantitative intersectional analyses in theories of power. <br>

<div class="section-divider"></div>

<p>
Moreover, the tool’s The <a href="reflexive/">self-reflexive</a> questions on power in knowledge production acknowledge that research itself is embedded in the very hierarchies it seeks to analyze (Collins/Bilge 2020, 194) – on the individual level and within institutional structures.
</p>
<p>
Critical reflection on how these power relations shape knowledge production is grounded in Black feminist epistemology, which emphasizes that standpoints are systematically structured through intersectional relations of power and that marginalized positions can generate distinctive analytical insights (Collins 2000). Reflexively engaging with and critically challenging these dynamics is therefore situated at the very heart of the intersectional paradigm.
</p>
