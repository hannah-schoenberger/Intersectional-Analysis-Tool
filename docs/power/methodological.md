<details class="question-accordion">

<summary>
<span class="dimension">Operationalization</span>
<span class="question">
How does the <i>operationalization</i> conceptualize the variables within power dynamics?
</span>
</summary>

<div class="accordion-body">

<div class="challenge-grid">

<div>

<h3>Methodological Challenges</h3>

<ul>

<li>
Socio-demographic and administrative variables are often naturalized and treated as self-explanatory.<sup class="citation">1</sup>
</li>

<li>
Databases lack complex power-sensitive variables. 
</li>

</ul>

</div>

<div>

<h3>Potential Approaches</h3>

<ul>

<li>
Linking variables to relevant power structures in theoretical conceptualization and interpretation. 

<li>
Critically reflecting on operationalization, e.g. limits of proxy variables.<sup class="citation">9,10</sup>
</li>

<li>
Considering categorization itself as an exercise of power.<sup class="citation">7,8</sup>
</li>

</ul>

</div>

</div>
</div>
</details>

<details class="question-accordion">

<summary>
<span class="dimension">Conception of Power</span>
<span class="question">
What <i>conception of power</i> underlies the analysis?
<br>
<br>
</span>

<div class="accordion-sub-question">
To what extent are <u>oppression and resistance</u> analysed?
</div>

<div class="accordion-sub-question">
To what extent are <u>marginalisation and privilege</u> considered?
</div>

</summary>

<div class="accordion-body">

<div class="challenge-grid">

<div>

<h3>Methodological Challenges</h3>

<ul>

<li>
Quantitative results are often perceived as objective facts, leading to weak theoretical grounding.<sup class="citation">2,3</sup>
</li>

<li>
Privileged social positions are often treated as the reference group, which can normalize privilege.<sup class="citation">4,5,6</sup>
</li>

<li>
Overlapping constellations of privileged and marginalized social positions are difficult to model and interpret.<sup class="citation">4</sup>
</li>

</ul>

</div>

<div>

<h3>Potential Approaches</h3>

<ul>

<li>
Making the understanding of power explicit and building on intersectional theory.<sup class="citation">11</sup>
</li>

<li>
Using MAIHDA to model marginalization and privilege.<sup class="citation">4</sup>
</li>

<li>
Considering alternative coding strategies to make privilege explicit.<sup class="citation">5</sup>
</li>

</ul>

</div>

<details class="reference-block">

<summary>Notes</summary>

<ol>

 The tool provides selected examples of methodological challenges identified in the literature on quantitative intersectional analysis, together with approaches proposed to address them. These examples are derived from the literature analysis underlying the development of the tool. While not exhaustive, they illustrate how the framework can be applied to systematically reflect on methodological concerns. Readers are encouraged to use them as a starting point for further methodological discussion and for expanding the analytical toolkit of (quantitative) intersectional research. Although the examples presented here primarily focus on quantitative approaches, an analogous application of the framework to qualitative research may also prove fruitful. 
</li>

</ol>
