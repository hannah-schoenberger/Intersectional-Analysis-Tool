# Social Inequality

<div class="notebook-page">

<div class="notebook-content">

<div class="hero-quote">
“Intersectionality rejects <span class="quote-ellipsis">[…]</span> notions that normalize inequality by depicting it as natural and inevitable. Instead, intersectionality points to the workings of power relations in producing social inequalities.”
<span class="quote-author">Collins (2019, 46)</span>
</div>

<p>
By making the contingent and dynamic production of inequality visible, intersectional analysis <u> resists its normalization or naturalization</u> (Collins 2019, 46) and challenges narratives that explain social inequality without reference to <u>power</u> dynamics.
</p>

<p>
However, descriptive approaches tend to leave the mechanisms of inequality in a black box, opening space for alternative explanations that neglect power relations (Bauer/Scheim 2019, 237) (e.g. biologization or meritocratic logics (Collins/Bilge 2020, 38, 203)).
</p>

<p>
The <a href="guiding/">guiding</a> and <a href="methodological/">methodological</a>  questions on inequality support a stronger theoretical grounding of quantitative intersectional analyses in theories of inequality. They encourage engagement with possible underlying <u>mechanisms</u>.
</p>
<div class="section-divider"></div>
<p>
The <a href="reflexive/">self-reflexive</a> questions on inequality also entail how dominant narratives of inequality (e.g., meritocratic, biologizing, or culturalist explanations) may shape researchers’ own assumptions, disciplinary traditions, and the ways results are perceived by the audience.
</p>
