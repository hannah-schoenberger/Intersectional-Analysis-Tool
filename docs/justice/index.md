# Social Justice

<div class="notebook-page">

<div class="notebook-content">

<div class="hero-quote">
“Intersectionality’s raison d’être is not simply to provide more complex and comprehensive explanations of how and why social inequalities persist, <span class="quote-ellipsis">[…]</span> leaving someone else to fix them. <span class="quote-ellipsis">[…]</span> <br>When it comes to social justice, intersectionality demands <span class="quote-ellipsis">[…]</span> turning critical analyses into critical praxis.”
<span class="quote-author">Collins/Bilge (2020, 202-203)</span>
</div>

<p>
Intersectional theory and praxis are inherently linked to a <u>commitment to social justice</u>. This normative dimension challenges the traditional boundaries between academic inquiry, activism, and social movements (Collins/Bilge 2020, 47, 202). However, the core concept of social justice has become increasingly contested within the intersectional field (Collins/Bilge 2020, 202), risking the loss of intersectionality’s transformative potential. In quantitative approaches, it is particularly challenged by the dominance of positivist epistemologies and the associated understanding of objectivity (Else-Quest/Hyde 2016a, 159ff.).
</p>

<p>
The <a href="guiding/">guiding</a> and <a href="methodological/">methodological</a>  questions therefore encourage researchers to clarify how concepts such as justice, equality, or equity are theoretically conceptualized, including their underlying epistemological premises. Furthermore, they encourage reflection on how research is positioned in relation to social justice praxis and how these commitments are translated into concrete research practices.
</p>

<p>
The <a href="reflexive/">self-reflexive</a> questions on social justice in knowledge production focus on the ethical and political implications of intersectional research and support the development of social justice–oriented research ethics (for best practice examples see e.g. Ahyoud et al. 2018, 33+38; Aikins et al. 2021, 33–34).
</p>
