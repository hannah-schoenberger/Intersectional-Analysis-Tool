<details class="question-accordion">

<summary>
<span class="dimension">'Addition' | Adding Categories</span>
<span class="question">
How might social inequality be modelled more precisely by <i>introducing new categories</i> and their combined effects?
</span>
</summary>

<div class="accordion-body">

<div class="challenge-grid">

<div>
<h3>Potential Approaches</h3>
<ul>
<li>Adding new predictors into multiple regression models.</li>
<br>
<br>
<br>
<br>
<br>
<br>
<br>
<br>
<br>
<br>
<br>
<li>Net-effect analyses: Examining relations between power systems indirectly through the social inequalities they produce (principle of explained variance).<sup class="citation">1</sup></li>
</ul>
</div>

<div>
<h3>Methodological Challenges</h3>
<ul>
<li>Partial regression coefficients assume that effects of distinct categories can be separated and hierarchized.<sup class="citation">2,3</sup></li>
<li>Main-effect predictors treat effects as parallel yet separable.<sup class="citation">2,3</sup></li>
<li>Main-effect equations can only model purely additive effects; interactions beyond this risk misinterpretation.<sup class="citation">4</sup></li>
<b>Models based solely on main effects are <u>not recommended</u> for intersectional analysis.</b>
<br>
<li>In net effect analyses (with or without interactions) mechanisms remain in a <u>black box</u> (descriptive approach).<sup class="citation">5,6,7,8,9</sup> 
</li>
<b> hard to develop targeted measures </b>
<b> Qualitative heterogeneity of mechanisms producing the same quantitative outcomes remains hidden.</b>

</ul>
</div>

</div>

<details class="question-accordion">

<summary>
<span class="dimension">'Addition' | Adding Interactions</span>

<div class="main-question">
How can <i>interactions</i> be modelled?</div>
<br>
<div class="accordion-sub-question"> How does the model/analysis account for the <u>different ways</u> one variable can change the effects of another? 
<span class="inline-handwritten"style="float: right"> ↳ e.g. summation, amplification, attenuation, reversal of effects... </span>  
  </div>


</summary>

<div class="accordion-body">

<div class="challenge-grid">

<div>
<h3>Potential Approaches</h3>
<ul>
<li>‘adding’ interaction terms or intersectional dummy variables into multiple regression models<sup class="citation">1,2,3</sup></li>
<br>
<br>
<br>
<li>Net-effect analyses: Examining relations between power systems indirectly through the social inequalities they produce (principle of explained variance).<sup class="citation">12</sup></li>

</ul>
</div>

<div>
<h3>Methodological Challenges</h3>
<ul>
<li>Higher-order interactions are difficult to interpret.<sup class="citation">9,10,11,4</sup></li>
<li>Exponentially increasing numbers of predictors increase the risk of overfitting (see <a href="/complexity/methodological">intercategorical complexity</a>.</li>
<br>
<li>In net effect analyses (with or without interactions) mechanisms remain in a <u>black box</u> (descriptive approach).<sup class="citation">5,6,7,8,9</sup> 
</li>
<b> hard to develop targeted measures </b>
<b> Qualitative heterogeneity of mechanisms producing the same quantitative outcomes remains hidden.</b>

</ul>
</div>

</div>

<summary>
<span class="dimension">Articulation</span>

<div class="main-question">
How can the black box of <i>mechanisms</i> be opened? </div>
<br>

<div class="accordion-sub-question">
How do the <u>power structures</u> become effective, (re)producing inequality? 
</div>
<div class="accordion-sub-question">
How exactly are the elements <u>dynamically connected</u>? 
</div>

</span>
</summary>

<div class="accordion-body">

<div class="challenge-grid">

<div>
<h3>Potential Approaches</h3>
<ul>
<li>Analytic approaches to identify mediators: Moderation, mediation, and path analyses <sup class="citation">1,2</sup></li>
<li>Measuring self-reported experiences of discrimination.<sup class="citation">4,5,6</sup></li>
<li>Complementing quantitative analyses with qualitative exploration of mechanisms.<sup class="citation">7</sup></li>
</ul>
</div>

<div>
<h3>Methodological Challenges</h3>
<ul>
<li>Operationalizations of discrimination often capture only additive effects.<sup class="citation">2,6,7</sup></li>
<li>Many mechanisms are difficult to observe directly.<sup class="citation">3,4,5</sup></li>
<li>Databases often lack suitable variables to operationalize mechanisms.
</ul>
</div>

<details class="question-accordion">

<summary>
<span class="dimension">Co-Formation</span>
<span class="question">
How do categories <i>mutually reconstruct</i> one another? <br> How do they shape each other's meanings and structures?
</span>
</summary>

<div class="accordion-body">

<div class="challenge-grid">

<div>
<h3>Potential Approaches</h3>
<ul>
<li>Interdependence analyses: Operationalizing power categories as dependent variables.<sup class="citation">1,2,3</sup></li>
<li>Analysing effect-measure modification (variation of survey instruments by social position)</li>
<li>Cross-categorical discrimination indices <sup class="citation">4</sup>
</ul>
</div>

<div>
<h3>Methodological Challenges</h3>
<ul>
<li>Effects of individual categories can no longer be considered separately.
<li>The anti-categorical nature of co-formation challenges conventional categorical thinking.<sup class="citation">5</sup></li>
<li>Very limited methodological experience and best-practice examples exist.
</ul>
</div>

</div>

<details class="reference-block">

<summary>Notes</summary>

<ol>
The tool provides selected examples of methodological challenges identified in the literature on quantitative intersectional analysis, together with approaches proposed to address them. These examples are derived from the literature analysis underlying the development of the tool. While not exhaustive, they illustrate how the framework can be applied to systematically reflect on methodological concerns. Readers are encouraged to use them as a starting point for further methodological discussion and for expanding the analytical toolkit of (quantitative) intersectional research. Although the examples presented here primarily focus on quantitative approaches, an analogous application of the framework to qualitative research may also prove fruitful.
</ol>
