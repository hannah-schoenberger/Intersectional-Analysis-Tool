<details class="question-accordion">

<summary>
<span class="dimension">Internal Contextualization</span>
<span class="question">
How does the analysis account for
<span class="annotated-word">
  <i>context-dependent variations</i>

  <span
    class="annotated-word-note">
    ↳ e.g. variations in distribution of social inequality, <br>
    variations in meaning,relevance and boundaries of categories/social positions,<br>
    variations in relational processes...
  </span>
  </span>
<u>within</u> the data?
<br>
<br>
</span>
</summary>

<div class="accordion-body">

<div class="challenge-grid">

<div>

<h3>Methodological Challenges</h3>

<ul>

<li>
Large datasets or pooled data contain high contextual heterogeneity, which creates a risk of overgeneralization.<sup class="citation">1,2</sup>
</li>

<li>
Secondary data may lack contextual variables. 
</li>

</ul>

</div>

<div>

<h3>Potential Approaches</h3>

<ul>

<li>
Comparative analysis of nested data, e.g. multilevel analysis.<sup class="citation">3,4</sup>
</li>

<li>
Incorporating contextual variables <sup class="citation">4</sup> <b>e.g. place or date of data collection</b>
</li>

<li>
Longitudinal pre-post designs <b>e.g. to evaluate organizational equality measures</b>
</li>

<li>
Transparent reporting and reflection on context-specific operationalization <sup class="citation">5,1</sup> <b>e.g. time- and country-dependent racialized categorization</b>
</li>

</ul>

</div>

</div>

<details class="question-accordion">

<summary>
<span class="dimension">External Contextualization</span>
<span class="question">
How did the <i>context of data collection</i> shape the data?
<br>
<br>
</span>
<div class="accordion-sub-question">
Which contexts are <u>represented or excluded</u>? What are the implications?
</div>
<div class="accordion-sub-question">
To what extent are findings <u>generalizable</u> beyond the immediate context, and what are context-specific particularities?
</div>
</summary>

<div class="accordion-body">

<div class="challenge-grid">

<div>

<h3>Methodological Challenges</h3>

<ul>

<li>
Marginalized social positions may be systematically under- or over-represented depending on context.<sup class="citation">3,4
<b>e.g. inaccessible questionnaires or racial profiling in criminal statistics</b>
</sup>
</li>

<li>
Representativeness can only be assessed for categories with reference data, but data gaps often exist for marginalized groups <sup class="citation">1,2</sup>
<b>e.g. on the distribution of gender diversity or the number of people affected by racism </b>
</li>

</ul>

</div>

<div>

<h3>Potential Approaches</h3>

<ul>

<li>
Transparent reporting on the conditions of data collection<sup class="citation">3,6</sup>
</li>

<li>
Critical reflection on dataset representativeness<sup class="citation">7</sup>: <br> - Specifying reference variables.
<br>
- Avoiding absolute claims of representativeness.
</li>

</ul>

</div>

<details class="reference-block">

<summary>Notes</summary>

<ol>

 The tool provides selected examples of methodological challenges identified in the literature on quantitative intersectional analysis, together with approaches proposed to address them. These examples are derived from the literature analysis underlying the development of the tool. While not exhaustive, they illustrate how the framework can be applied to systematically reflect on methodological concerns. Readers are encouraged to use them as a starting point for further methodological discussion and for expanding the analytical toolkit of (quantitative) intersectional research. Although the examples presented here primarily focus on quantitative approaches, an analogous application of the framework to qualitative research may also prove fruitful. 
</li>

</ol>
