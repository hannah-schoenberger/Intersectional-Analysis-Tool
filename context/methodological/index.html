    Internal Contextualization How does the analysis account for context-dependent variations ↳ e.g. variations in distribution of social inequality,
    variations in meaning,relevance and boundaries of categories/social positions,
    variations in relational processes...     within the data?

    Methodological Challenges

    • Large datasets or pooled data contain high contextual heterogeneity, which creates a risk of overgeneralization.1,2
    • Secondary data may lack contextual variables.

    Potential Approaches

    • Comparative analysis of nested data, e.g. multilevel analysis.3,4
    • Incorporating contextual variables 4 e.g. place or date of data collection
    • Longitudinal pre-post designs e.g. to evaluate organizational equality measures
    • Transparent reporting and reflection on context-specific operationalization 5,1 e.g. time- and country-dependent racialized categorization

    External Contextualization How did the context of data collection shape the data?

    Which contexts are represented or excluded? What are the implications?
    To what extent are findings generalizable beyond the immediate context, and what are context-specific particularities?

    Methodological Challenges

    • Marginalized social positions may be systematically under- or over-represented depending on context.3,4 e.g. inaccessible questionnaires or racial profiling in criminal statistics
    • Representativeness can only be assessed for categories with reference data, but data gaps often exist for marginalized groups 1,2 e.g. on the distribution of gender diversity or the number of people affected by racism

    Potential Approaches

    • Transparent reporting on the conditions of data collection3,6
    • Critical reflection on dataset representativeness7:
      - Specifying reference variables.
      - Avoiding absolute claims of representativeness.

    Notes
      The tool provides selected examples of methodological challenges identified in the literature on quantitative intersectional analysis, together with approaches proposed to address them. These examples are derived from the literature analysis underlying the development of the tool. While not exhaustive, they illustrate how the framework can be applied to systematically reflect on methodological concerns. Readers are encouraged to use them as a starting point for further methodological discussion and for expanding the analytical toolkit of (quantitative) intersectional research. Although the examples presented here primarily focus on quantitative approaches, an analogous application of the framework to qualitative research may also prove fruitful.
