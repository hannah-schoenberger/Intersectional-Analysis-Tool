    Social Context

    “This theme […] informs differences within intersectionality itself”
    “thinking about social inequalities and power relations […] not in abstract generalizations but in their specific contexts” Collins/Bilge (2020, 196&200)

    The intersectional paradigm acknowledges that power structures, inequalities, and their complex relations are context-dependent (Collins/Bilge 2020, 196): The same intersection may exhibit different distributions of social inequality1, different relations between elements of power, and different relevant categories.2

    The guiding and methodological questions on social context therefore encourage researchers to examine context effects within datasets (internal contextualization) (Bowleg/Bauer 2016, 338) as well as situating the data within the research’s context (external contextualization) (Else-Quest/Hyde 2016a, 164). However, analyses should consider both differences and similarities across contexts, in order to avoid naturalizing distinctions — particularly between nation states — and to remain attentive to overarching processes (e.g. transnational dynamics) (Collins/Bilge 2020, 195).

    Speaking from the standpoint-theoretical understanding outlined above, applying the core concept of social context reflexively can be understood as locating the standpoint of knowledge production within the field of intersectionality itself as well as within broader social contexts. The self-reflexive questions on social context in knowledge production therefore address the disciplinary contexts influencing the analysis (e.g. epistemology, terminology, research protocols, conception of theory) as well as the contexts that have shaped the researchers’ understanding of intersectionality. This includes, for example, academic training, the bodies of literature informing the research process, and experiential knowledge emerging from political engagement, activism, or intersectional praxis (Collins/Bilge 2020, 197; Moradi 2017, 500.).

    Notes
    1. For an empirical example, see McCall's (2001) study of wage inequality, which compares different economic contexts in US cities.
    2. In their overview of the reclassification of racialised variables in the US Census, the Pew Research Center (2020) provides a graphic example of how metacategorical and positional complexity depend on the temporal context.
