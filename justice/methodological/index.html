    Theoretical Conceptualization How is justice theoretically conceptualized?
    How are social inequalities normatively evaluated?

    Methodological Challenges

    • Positivist epistemology emphasizes value-neutrality, challenging the normative dimension of social justice.1

    Potential Approaches

    • Providing a clear definition and conceptualization of justice e.g. equity vs. equality vs. justice
    • Naming and acknowledging injustice.
    Epistemology and Normativity Which epistemological paradigm underlies the research?
    What assumptions about objectivity are made?
    How is the relationship between science and normative/political praxis understood?

    Methodological Challenges

    • Positivist epistemology emphasizes value-neutrality, challenging the normative dimension of social justice.1

    Potential Approaches

    • Reflecting on the epistemological assumptions underlying the research.1
    Social Justice Praxis How are research findings disseminated to inform social justice projects?
    How can the research contribute to actionable interventions or policy recommendations?

    Methodological Challenges

    • Controversy over whether quantitative research can actively engage with social justice.2,3

    Potential Approaches

    • Developing strategies for theory–praxis transfer and dissemination beyond academia.4
    • Empowerment through knowledge (e.g. participatory action research).1,5,6
    • Analysis and evaluation of social justice praxis as an object of inquiry.1

    Notes
      The tool provides selected examples of methodological challenges identified in the literature on quantitative intersectional analysis, together with approaches proposed to address them. These examples are derived from the literature analysis underlying the development of the tool. While not exhaustive, they illustrate how the framework can be applied to systematically reflect on methodological concerns. Readers are encouraged to use them as a starting point for further methodological discussion and for expanding the analytical toolkit of (quantitative) intersectional research. Although the examples presented here primarily focus on quantitative approaches, an analogous application of the framework to qualitative research may also prove fruitful.
