    'Addition' | Adding Categories How might social inequality be modelled more precisely by introducing new categories and their combined effects?

    Potential Approaches

    • Adding new predictors into multiple regression models.











    • Net-effect analyses: Examining relations between power systems indirectly through the social inequalities they produce (principle of explained variance).1

    Methodological Challenges

    • Partial regression coefficients assume that effects of distinct categories can be separated and hierarchized.2,3
    • Main-effect predictors treat effects as parallel yet separable.2,3
    • Main-effect equations can only model purely additive effects; interactions beyond this risk misinterpretation.4
      • Models based solely on main effects are not recommended for intersectional analysis.
    • In net effect analyses (with or without interactions) mechanisms remain in a black box (descriptive approach).5,6,7,8,9
      • hard to develop targeted measures Qualitative heterogeneity of mechanisms producing the same quantitative outcomes remains hidden.

    'Addition' | Adding Interactions
    How can interactions be modelled?

    How does the model/analysis account for the different ways one variable can change the effects of another? ↳ e.g. summation, amplification, attenuation, reversal of effects...

    Potential Approaches

    • ‘adding’ interaction terms or intersectional dummy variables into multiple regression models1,2,3



    • Net-effect analyses: Examining relations between power systems indirectly through the social inequalities they produce (principle of explained variance).12

    Methodological Challenges

    • Higher-order interactions are difficult to interpret.9,10,11,4
    • Exponentially increasing numbers of predictors increase the risk of overfitting (see intercategorical complexity.

    • In net effect analyses (with or without interactions) mechanisms remain in a black box (descriptive approach).5,6,7,8,9
      • hard to develop targeted measures Qualitative heterogeneity of mechanisms producing the same quantitative outcomes remains hidden.

    Articulation
    How can the black box of mechanisms be opened?

    How do the power structures become effective, (re)producing inequality?
    How exactly are the elements dynamically connected?

    Potential Approaches

    • Analytic approaches to identify mediators: Moderation, mediation, and path analyses 1,2
    • Measuring self-reported experiences of discrimination.4,5,6
    • Complementing quantitative analyses with qualitative exploration of mechanisms.7

    Methodological Challenges

    • Operationalizations of discrimination often capture only additive effects.2,6,7
    • Many mechanisms are difficult to observe directly.3,4,5
    • Databases often lack suitable variables to operationalize mechanisms.

    Co-Formation How do categories mutually reconstruct one another?
    How do they shape each other's meanings and structures?

    Potential Approaches

    • Interdependence analyses: Operationalizing power categories as dependent variables.1,2,3
    • Analysing effect-measure modification (variation of survey instruments by social position)
    • Cross-categorical discrimination indices 4

    Methodological Challenges

    • Effects of individual categories can no longer be considered separately.
    • The anti-categorical nature of co-formation challenges conventional categorical thinking.5
    • Very limited methodological experience and best-practice examples exist.

    Notes
      The tool provides selected examples of methodological challenges identified in the literature on quantitative intersectional analysis, together with approaches proposed to address them. These examples are derived from the literature analysis underlying the development of the tool. While not exhaustive, they illustrate how the framework can be applied to systematically reflect on methodological concerns. Readers are encouraged to use them as a starting point for further methodological discussion and for expanding the analytical toolkit of (quantitative) intersectional research. Although the examples presented here primarily focus on quantitative approaches, an analogous application of the framework to qualitative research may also prove fruitful.
